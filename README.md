# India → USA Electronics Export

A personal knowledge base and working document repository for building an electronics export business from India to the United States.

---

## What This Is

This repo tracks my research, planning, and execution as I explore transitioning from an IT career into cross-border electronics trade. The focus is specifically on sourcing electronics from Indian manufacturers and getting them into the U.S. market through three capital-light strategies: acting as a **sourcing agent** for U.S. companies, **B2B wholesale**, and **white-label** selling on Amazon. Private label is deliberately out of scope (too capital- and time-heavy for a side-business start).

This is not a theoretical exercise. The goal is a real, operating business.

---

## Project Goals

- **Start cash-positive** with the sourcing-agent model (Model D) — land first U.S. clients with little to no capital at risk
- **Build a vetted supplier bench** of compliant, export-ready Indian manufacturers across 2–3 focus categories
- **Validate** product categories where India has a genuine cost/quality edge (and a real non-Section-301 duty advantage, verified per HTS)
- **Prove unit economics honestly** — judge by net margin (after all landed costs, fees, PPC, returns), not gross
- **Expand** into B2B wholesale and/or white-label Amazon selling once supplier relationships and demand are proven
- **Transition** from IT career to full-time operator once the business hits ~$15K+/month in consistent revenue

---

## Guiding Principles

- **Compliance first.** No shortcuts on FCC, UL/ETL, or CPSC. One seizure or account suspension can kill the business before it starts.
- **India's non-Section-301 status is the structural edge.** Use it. Avoid categories where Chinese sellers have unbeatable commodity lock-in.
- **Start small, learn fast.** First order is a market research investment, not a profit center.
- **Side business until the data says otherwise.** IT income funds the experiment without debt pressure.

## How the Paths Fit Together (Progression Map)

These aren't four separate businesses — they're stages of one, ordered by capital and risk. Each funds and de-risks the next.

```
   START                                                          ENDGAME
   ┌─────────┐      ┌──────────────┐      ┌──────────────┐      ┌──────────────┐
   │ D        │ ───▶ │ C            │ ───▶ │ B            │ ───▶ │ A            │
   │ Sourcing │      │ B2B          │      │ White Label  │      │ Private      │
   │ Agent    │      │ Wholesale    │      │ (Amazon)     │      │ Label        │
   └─────────┘      └──────────────┘      └──────────────┘      └──────────────┘
   ~$0 capital       inventory+terms       inventory+PPC          $15k–40k+/SKU
   cash in weeks     recurring, sticky      B2C reach/margin       margin + moat
```

- **Start: D (Sourcing Agent)** — near-zero capital, cash in weeks. Use it to build a vetted supplier bench and learn the chain. Structure it around *project fees and buy/sell deals* because pure commission gets disintermediated.
- **D → C (B2B Wholesale)** — when a category shows repeatable demand, stock it and sell on your own invoice. The buy/sell structure also solves D's disintermediation problem. The durable home for a sourcing relationship.
- **D/C → B (White Label)** — when a *consumer* product shows demand, launch your own Amazon SKU using the supplier and cash you've built. The most competitive channel — enter funded, not cold.
- **B → A (Private Label)** — only after a white-label SKU is a *proven* net-margin winner. Then commit capital to your own brand to capture premium pricing and a defensible, sellable asset.

You can run tracks in **parallel** (e.g., D for cash while C builds), but capital flows *forward* along this chain — validate cheap, commit expensive last. Each model doc has its own **"central challenges & how to deal"** and **"when to move"** sections.
