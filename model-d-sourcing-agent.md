# Model D Playbook: Sourcing Agent / Trading Partner for U.S. Companies

> **What this is:** You help U.S. companies get electronics (and electronic components/assemblies) manufactured or sourced in India, and you earn a commission or markup. You hold little or no inventory and usually are **not** the Importer of Record. This is the lowest-capital, lowest-risk way to start — and it fits your profile (U.S.-based, IT-literate, fluent in Indian supplier dynamics) better than any other model.

> All numbers here are illustrative. Verify duties, compliance, and current rules per product. See [business-plan.md](business-plan.md) for shared compliance/customs detail.

---

## Why this fits you specifically

- **No inventory, no customs liability.** Your client is usually the IOR. You're the matchmaker + quality/process layer.
- **Your IT background is the product.** Indian factories are strong on manufacturing, weak on systems: documentation, version control on specs, structured QA reporting, responsive English communication, project tracking. You sell *reliability and process*, not just a cheaper quote.
- **You arbitrage trust and time zones.** A U.S. buyer doesn't want to vet 15 IndiaMart suppliers, manage a 10.5-hour time gap, or decode "yes" that means "maybe." You do that for them.
- **Cash-positive fast.** No $10k inventory bet. Your costs are time, a website, travel, and sample shipping.

---

## ⚠️ The central challenge: disintermediation (read before you fall in love with this)

A sourcing agent who *just makes an introduction* and collects a commission is the easiest role in the chain to delete. Both sides are motivated to cut you out:
- The **buyer** thinks "why pay 8% forever when I can email the factory directly after order one?"
- The **factory** thinks "why share margin when I can sell to this U.S. buyer directly?" Indian and Chinese factories routinely reach around agents.

And the risk is worst exactly when it matters: **it scales with order size and frequency.** Small, occasional orders aren't worth the hassle of going direct. Large recurring programs are — and by then the buyer has both the incentive *and* the competence (they've learned the factory, the process, the time-zone dance) to remove you. **A pure agent tends to get cut out precisely when the account becomes valuable.** Non-circumvention agreements (NCNDA) exist but are weak in practice — slow and expensive to enforce across borders against a small client. Treat them as deterrent theater, not protection.

**Conclusion: "land a client, collect a perpetual commission" is mostly a fantasy.** This model earns its place as a *bootstrap*, not a forever-business.

### How to actually defend against it (ranked by how real the protection is)

1. **Be the seller of record, not the introducer (buy/sell markup).** Buy from the factory, sell to the client on *your* invoice; the client may never see the factory's name. Nothing to circumvent. **But note the tradeoff:** this turns you from an asset-light agent into a small trading company taking on inventory and payment risk — i.e., it converts you toward [Model C](model-c-b2b-wholesale.md). The asset-light pitch and the defensibility are in tension.
2. **Own QC, logistics, and financing.** If you inspect every batch, consolidate shipments, handle the import, and float terms, going direct means the buyer takes all that risk back in-house. Many won't — as long as the hassle you remove exceeds your fee.
3. **Manage a multi-supplier program, not one SKU.** Orchestrating five factories and being the single point of accountability is hard to delete. One product from one factory is trivial to delete.
4. **Add engineering/compliance value** the factory can't — spec development, FCC/UL/CPSC navigation, DFM feedback. This is where your IT/systems background is genuinely sticky.
5. **Favor project-based flat fees over recurring commission.** Get paid *before* disintermediation is even possible (see the "Sourcing Sprint" offer below), rather than betting on a recurring cut you'll probably lose.

## How you get paid (pick per deal)

| Model | How it works | Typical range | Best when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Commission on order value** | You invoice the buyer (or the factory pays you) a % of PO value | 5–12% | Standard finished-goods sourcing |
| **Buy/sell markup** | You buy from factory, sell to client at a markup (you're briefly in the middle) | 8–20% gross | Client wants one clean invoice and won't deal with India directly |
| **Retainer + success fee** | Monthly retainer ($1.5k–$5k) plus a smaller % | retainer + 3–6% | Ongoing programs, multiple SKUs |
| **Per-project sourcing fee** | Flat fee to find + vet + sample a qualified supplier | $2k–$10k/project | One-off "find me a factory for X" |
| **QC / inspection add-on** | Per-inspection fee on top of sourcing | $200–$500/inspection | Client wants you to own quality |

**Practical tip:** Start with **buy/sell markup or commission** to land your first clients (lower friction, no retainer ask), then move proven clients onto **retainer + success fee** for predictable revenue.

## Setup (1–2 weeks, under ~$1,000)

1. **Entity + basics:** U.S. LLC, EIN, business bank account (Mercury), business email on your own domain. You already know this from your IT work.
2. **Positioning one-liner.** e.g., *"I help U.S. electronics and hardware companies manufacture in India — vetted factories, U.S.-based point of contact, and engineering-grade documentation and QA."*
3. **A real website (1 page is fine).** Sections: who you help, what you do, why India (and your non-Section-301 cost angle), your process, and a "book a call" button (Calendly). Your IT skills make this a weekend job.
4. **Supplier bench.** Before you pitch a single client, qualify **5–10 factories** across 2–3 categories you'll focus on (e.g., wired audio, LED/lighting, cable assemblies, PCB/PCBA, enclosures). Use the verification checklist in [business-plan.md §6](business-plan.md). You want to credibly say "I already have vetted partners for X."
5. **A one-page capability deck** per focus category: what India does well, MOQ ranges, lead times, sample certs you've already verified. This is your sales asset.
6. **A simple CRM + pipeline.** Airtable or a spreadsheet: company, contact, category, stage, next action, PO value. Treat lead-gen like an IT project with a tracked backlog.

## How to generate business (this is the core)

You need a steady flow of U.S. companies that buy or could buy India-made electronics/hardware. Run several channels in parallel; track reply and call-booked rates per channel.

### Channel 1 — Mine import records (highest-signal cold outreach)
U.S. ocean import data is public via bill-of-lading records. Tools like **ImportYeti (free), Panjiva, ImportGenius, Datamyne** let you search **who is already importing electronics from India or China.**
- **Companies importing from India already** = warm: they believe in India sourcing; you pitch *better execution*.
- **Companies importing the same category from China** = your best target: pitch the **Section 301 cost edge** of switching a SKU to India (verify the duty delta per HTS first).
- Pull a list of 100–200 companies, find the buyer/ops/procurement contact on LinkedIn, and run targeted outreach (below).

### Channel 2 — LinkedIn outreach to the right titles
Target titles: **Procurement Manager, Sourcing Manager, Supply Chain Manager, Director of Operations, Hardware/Product Engineering Lead, Founder (for small hardware startups).**
- Company profiles: SMB hardware brands, electronics importers, contract distributors, hardware startups (often desperate for a sourcing partner), lighting companies, accessory brands.
- Cadence: ~10–15 personalized messages/day. Personalize line 1 with something real (their product, a recent launch, the fact that they import from China).

**Sample LinkedIn message:**
> Hi {Name} — I help U.S. {hardware/electronics} companies manufacture in India with vetted factories and a U.S.-based point of contact, so you're not chasing suppliers across a 10-hour time gap. I noticed {company} sells {product} — for several SKUs, moving production to India avoids the China Section 301 surcharge entirely. Worth a 15-min call to see if any of your SKUs are a fit?

### Channel 3 — Hardware startups (underserved and fast-moving)
Hardware startups need manufacturing help and rarely have a sourcing function.
- **Where they are:** Y Combinator / accelerator demo days, Kickstarter & Indiegogo electronics projects (these founders *need* a manufacturer **right now**), r/hardware, Hardware-focused Slack/Discord communities, Hax/Han Hai and other hardware accelerators.
- **Crowdfunding play:** Filter Kickstarter/Indiegogo for funded electronics campaigns. A funded creator has money and an urgent manufacturing problem. Reach out the week they hit their goal.

### Channel 4 — Trade shows (both sides of the ocean)
- **U.S. side (find clients):** CES, Electronica-adjacent U.S. events, LightFair (lighting), NAMM (if audio), industry-specific shows for your category. Walk the floor and talk to product/ops people, not just sales.
- **India side (deepen supplier bench + credibility):** India Electronics Week (Bengaluru), ELCINA events, component shows. Photos of you on factory floors are powerful trust signals in your outreach.

### Channel 5 — Content + inbound (compounds over time, plays to your strengths)
Publish where buyers search:
- LinkedIn posts and a simple blog: *"How to move an electronics SKU from China to India without blowing up your duty bill,"* *"What CPSC eFiling (July 2026) means for small importers,"* *"A checklist for vetting an Indian electronics factory."*
- A lead magnet: **"India Electronics Sourcing Checklist (PDF)"** behind an email capture.
- This positions you as the expert and generates inbound calls. Your IT/writing ability makes this cheap to produce.

### Channel 6 — Referral + partner network
- **Customs brokers and freight forwarders** talk to importers all day — befriend a couple; they refer sourcing work and you refer import work back.
- **U.S. CPAs/attorneys who serve importers**, fractional ops consultants, and **Indian export-promotion councils** (e.g., ELCINA, EEPC India) can route leads.
- Every happy client → ask for one intro. B2B referrals close far faster than cold.

### Channel 7 — Productized "find me a factory" offer
List a concrete, flat-fee service (e.g., on your site, and where founders hang out): *"India Sourcing Sprint — in 3 weeks I deliver 3 vetted, sample-verified factories for your product, fixed fee $X."* Low commitment for the client, fast cash for you, and it converts into ongoing commission/retainer work.

## The delivery workflow (what you actually do once you land a client)

```
1. INTAKE        → Lock the spec: product, target cost, MOQ, certs needed (FCC/UL/CPSC/DOE?),
                   packaging, timeline. Your IT discipline shines here — version the spec doc.
2. SHORTLIST     → Pull 3–5 vetted factories from your bench (or source new ones). Verify IEC,
                   GSTIN, real factory, export history, certificate numbers.
3. RFQ + SAMPLES → Standardized RFQ to all; collect quotes at 100/500/1k tiers; order samples.
4. EVALUATE      → Side-by-side comparison for the client: price, quality, compliance, lead time,
                   risk. This report IS your value — make it clean and decision-ready.
5. NEGOTIATE     → Terms (30/70, never 100% advance), IP/tooling ownership, who is IOR (client).
6. QC + SHIP     → Pre-shipment inspection (you or SGS/QIMA/Bureau Veritas). Client's broker
                   files entry; you stay the single point of contact.
7. CLOSE LOOP    → Post-delivery review, fix issues, set up the reorder. Convert to retainer.
```

## First-30-days action plan

- **Week 1:** Entity/site/positioning. Pick 2–3 focus categories. Pull a 100-company import-records list.
- **Week 2:** Qualify 5+ factories for your bench. Build the RFQ template and one capability deck.
- **Week 3:** Launch outreach — 15 LinkedIn touches/day + 20 import-records emails/day. Publish 2 LinkedIn posts. Post your productized "Sourcing Sprint" offer.
- **Week 4:** Book and run discovery calls. Target: **3–5 calls, 1 paid Sourcing Sprint or pilot PO.** Refine pitch based on objections heard.

## Risks and mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **Disintermediation** (client + factory cut you out) | Be the indispensable process/QC layer, not just an intro; sign a sourcing agreement; hold the relationship and the QA. Move to retainer so you're paid for ongoing value, not a one-time intro. |
| **Factory quality drift on reorders** | You own QC; inspect every batch; keep a backup factory per category. |
| **Client non-payment / scope creep** | Deposits on project work; clear SOW; milestone billing. |
| **You misrepresent compliance** | Never claim a cert you haven't verified; route binding compliance to accredited labs and the client's broker. Your reputation is the whole business. |
| **Thin pipeline** | Always be running ≥3 lead channels; treat lead-gen as a standing weekly commitment, not a burst. |

## Why start here — and when to move on

Model D can be **cash-positive in weeks**, needs almost no capital, teaches you the entire India→U.S. supply chain on someone else's dime, and builds the exact supplier bench and market knowledge you'd need for the other models. That's why it's the rational *first* move — **as a bootstrap, structured around project fees and buy/sell deals, with eyes open about disintermediation.**

**When to move on:**
- **D → [C (B2B wholesale)](model-c-b2b-wholesale.md):** the moment you spot a category with *repeatable* demand from your sourcing work. Convert the relationship into buy/sell — stock the product and sell it on your own invoice. This is the durable endgame: it solves disintermediation by removing the factory from the buyer's view. Your instinct that C is sturdier than D is correct.
- **D → [B (white label)](model-b-white-label.md):** if your sourcing work surfaces a *consumer* product with demand, use the vetted supplier to launch your own Amazon SKU and capture B2C margin.
- **Don't try to scale D as a pure agency.** Past a handful of clients, a commission-only agent hits a ceiling and a rising disintermediation rate. Scaling means becoming a trading company (C) or a brand (B/A) — i.e., owning inventory and the customer relationship.

Think of D as the **on-ramp that funds and de-risks** the inventory models — not the destination.
