# Model C Playbook: B2B Wholesale / Distributor

> **What this is:** You import India-made electronics in bulk and sell to U.S. businesses — retailers, installers, distributors, resellers, and institutional buyers (schools, facilities). Lower per-unit margin than B2C, but larger orders, far fewer consumer returns, no Amazon-suspension roulette, and stickier relationships. You **are** the Importer of Record here, so compliance is fully on you.

> All numbers illustrative. Verify duties/compliance per product. Shared customs/compliance detail (incl. **CPSC eFiling effective July 8, 2026**) is in [business-plan.md](business-plan.md).

---

## Why this can fit you

- **Relationship + process selling** rewards your strengths over flashy consumer marketing.
- **Returns are minimal** vs. Amazon B2C — businesses buy deliberately and keep what works.
- **No marketplace dependency** — you're not one policy change away from a dead business.
- **India's edge is strongest in the categories B2B buyers want:** industrial/commercial LED, cable assemblies, components/PCBA, enclosures, STEM/education hardware.

**The catch:** you carry inventory and IOR liability, sales cycles are longer (weeks to months), and you must earn trust before a buyer commits to volume. This is a *build-relationships* game, not a *launch-and-wait* game.

---

## Best categories for B2B (India-advantaged, low consumer-return risk)

| Category | Who buys it | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| **Industrial / commercial LED fixtures** | Electrical contractors, distributors, facilities, warehouses | India very competitive; no Amazon race-to-bottom; UL/ETL + possible DOE |
| **Cable & wire harness assemblies** | OEMs, integrators, electronics resellers | India strong; low compliance for bare assemblies |
| **PCB / PCBA (assembled boards)** | U.S. hardware companies, repair shops | Very strong India advantage; B2B-only |
| **STEM / education electronics kits** | School districts, makerspaces, education distributors | Grant-funded budgets; recurring seasonal demand |
| **Mains accessories (power strips, drivers, supplies)** | Distributors, installers | Needs UL/ETL — your compliance becomes a selling point |

---

## Economics (illustrative)

- **Gross margin:** ~15–30%; **net ~8–20%** after freight, duty, warehousing, sales cost, and bad-debt reserve.
- **Order sizes:** far larger than B2C — a single contractor or distributor PO can dwarf months of Amazon sales.
- **Terms reality:** business buyers often expect **Net 30/60**. That means *you* finance the gap between paying your Indian supplier and getting paid. Model this carefully — it's the #1 cash-flow trap in B2B.

## Setup

1. **Entity, EIN, bank, customs bond, customs broker** (you're IOR — see [business-plan.md §5](business-plan.md)).
2. **Resale certificate / sales-tax registration** in your state (B2B sales are often tax-exempt with a resale cert — collect your buyers' certs).
3. **Compliance done right** for your category (UL/ETL, CPSC GCC, DOE if applicable). For B2B, **compliance is a sales asset** — contractors and distributors *require* listed products.
4. **Sales collateral:** one-page line sheet/spec sheet per product (specs, certs, pricing tiers, MOQ, lead time), a simple website, and professionally packaged samples.
5. **Small proof-of-concept inventory** or a drop-ship/just-in-time arrangement with your factory so you can fulfill a first PO without a huge upfront bet.

## How to generate business (the core)

B2B is outbound + relationships. Run these in parallel and track them like a sales pipeline.

### Channel 1 — Direct outreach to the buying businesses
Identify and contact the specific business type for your product:
- **For commercial LED:** electrical contractors, lighting designers, facility managers, ESCOs (energy service companies), property-management firms, distributors (Graybar, Rexel, Wesco — start with regional/independent distributors who are more approachable than the giants).
- **For components/PCBA:** small/mid U.S. hardware manufacturers, contract assemblers, electronics repair shops.
- **For STEM kits:** school district purchasing departments, education resellers, makerspaces, after-school program operators.

**How to find contacts:** LinkedIn Sales Navigator (filter by industry + title + region), Google Maps for local contractors/installers, industry association directories, and import-records tools (ImportYeti/Panjiva) to find businesses already importing similar goods.

**Sample cold email (commercial LED → contractor/distributor):**
> Subject: India-made commercial LED, UL-listed, no China tariff
>
> Hi {Name}, I supply UL-listed commercial LED {fixtures/high-bays} manufactured in India — comparable spec to what you're buying now, typically landed lower because India isn't subject to the China Section 301 tariff. I can send a spec sheet and a sample for you to evaluate on your next project. Would a quick call this week be useful?

### Channel 2 — Distributor / rep network
- **Manufacturer's reps** already sell into your target buyers and work on commission — recruiting 1–2 good reps per region multiplies your reach without fixed payroll. Find them via rep-finder directories (e.g., ERA — Electronics Representatives Association) and category associations.
- Pitch **regional distributors** on carrying your line; they handle the last-mile relationships, you handle import + supply.

### Channel 3 — Trade shows (where B2B actually closes)
Walk and exhibit at the shows your buyers attend:
- **LightFair** (architectural/commercial lighting), **NECA** (electrical contractors), category-specific shows.
- **For STEM:** ISTE, regional education-technology conferences, state school-board purchasing events.
- A modest booth + samples + a tight line sheet generates more qualified B2B leads than months of cold email. Even attending (not exhibiting) lets you meet buyers face-to-face.

### Channel 4 — Government & institutional procurement
- **Register on SAM.gov** to sell to federal buyers; register with **state/local and school-district procurement portals**.
- Watch bid boards for relevant solicitations. Institutional demand is steady, budget-backed, and less price-shopped — but paperwork-heavy (a fit for your systems mindset).
- Consider whether any **small-business set-aside** or supplier-diversity programs apply to you.

### Channel 5 — B2B marketplaces & directories
- List on **Faire, Amazon Business, Alibaba (as a U.S.-based seller), ThomasNet, Global Sources**, and category directories. These produce inbound wholesale inquiries with less effort than cold outreach.

### Channel 6 — Sample-led selling
The B2B close almost always runs through a sample. Make it easy: a clean **"request a free/low-cost evaluation sample"** offer on your site and in every outreach. A contractor who installs your sample fixture and likes it becomes a repeat-PO buyer.

### Channel 7 — Land-and-expand existing accounts
Cheapest revenue is the next order from a happy buyer. After a first PO: check in, fix any issues fast, introduce adjacent SKUs, set up standing reorders, and ask for a referral to peers (contractors and distributors all know each other).

## Sales workflow

```
1. PROSPECT   → Build a target list (business type + decision-maker).
2. OPEN       → Cold email/call/LinkedIn with the cost+compliance angle.
3. SAMPLE     → Get a spec'd, certified sample into their hands.
4. QUOTE      → Tiered pricing + MOQ + lead time; clarify terms (deposit vs. Net 30).
5. PILOT PO   → Small first order to prove reliability.
6. FULFILL    → Import (you're IOR), QC, deliver on time — reliability is everything in B2B.
7. EXPAND     → Reorders, more SKUs, referrals, standing supply agreement.
```

---

## First-90-days plan

- **Month 1:** Entity + compliance for one category; qualify the Indian supplier; build line sheet + samples; build a 100-account target list.
- **Month 2:** Outreach engine live (email + LinkedIn + 1 trade show or local-contractor canvassing); get samples into 10–20 buyers' hands; register on SAM.gov / relevant portals + 1–2 B2B marketplaces.
- **Month 3:** Convert samples to pilot POs; fulfill flawlessly; line up reorders and recruit a first manufacturer's rep.

---

## Metrics

- Target accounts contacted → samples placed
- Samples placed → pilot POs (this is the key conversion)
- Pilot POs → repeat/standing accounts
- Average PO value and reorder rate
- **Days sales outstanding (DSO)** — watch this like a hawk; Net 30/60 can strangle cash

## Risks and mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **Cash-flow squeeze from Net 30/60 terms** | Require deposits on first orders; offer small early-pay discounts; build a cash buffer; consider invoice factoring only once volume justifies it. |
| **IOR/compliance liability is fully yours** | Get real UL/ETL/CPSC/DOE; keep certificate data ready for **CPSC eFiling (July 8, 2026)**; use a customs broker. |
| **Long sales cycles** | Keep a wide pipeline; don't bet on one whale; use sample-led selling to shorten cycles. |
| **Buyer concentration** | Don't let one account exceed ~25–30% of revenue. |
| **Supplier reliability on bigger volumes** | Qualify a backup factory; inspect every batch; build lead-time buffers. |
| **Bad debt** | Credit-check large buyers; deposits; don't extend big terms to unproven accounts. |
